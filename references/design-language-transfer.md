# Design-Language Transfer

Use this reference when the user asks for an original website "in the style of" another site, brand, or extracted `DESIGN.md`.

The goal is to transfer design logic into a new business context, not to disguise a replica as original work.

## Inputs

- Reference URL, evidence, screenshots, or `DESIGN.md`.
- New site purpose, audience, owner, content, and desired action.
- New site's owned imagery, identity, and legal constraints.

When content is missing and the user asks for a demonstration, create clearly replaceable fictional content and use reserved contact details rather than a real third party's identity.

## Transfer Matrix

### Usually transferable

- Typography scale, weight relationships, and line-length discipline.
- Spacing rhythm and section density.
- Color roles and contrast relationships, with new site-specific values where needed.
- Navigation height, translucency, and responsive collapse behavior.
- Component proportions, border treatment, and interaction restraint.
- Full-width versus constrained layout patterns.
- Image scale, crop behavior, and editorial/product storytelling.
- Motion timing and reduced-motion behavior.

### Must be replaced or independently licensed

- Logos, trademarks, product names, and branded symbols.
- Source copy, slogans, campaign concepts, and distinctive messaging.
- Product photography, illustrations, video, and icon artwork.
- Data, testimonials, client names, metrics, and personal identity claims.
- A source page's exact section sequence when the new business goal requires another structure.

## Workflow

1. Write a transfer brief with `retain`, `reinterpret`, and `replace` decisions.
2. Build the new information architecture around the new site's audience and conversion goal.
3. Translate reference tokens into semantic tokens for the new site.
4. Produce original copy and imagery, or label demonstration content as replaceable.
5. Implement full responsive behavior, interactions, focus states, reduced motion, and real navigation destinations.
6. Validate asset URLs and inspect desktop, tablet, and mobile layouts.
7. Compare the result to the transfer brief, not to the source page's item counts.

## Example: Premium Product Homepage to Personal Portfolio

Retain:

- Quiet translucent navigation.
- Large but disciplined display typography.
- Product-first photography and sparse copy.
- Full-width light/dark bands and compact blue actions.
- Explicit desktop/mobile sizes instead of viewport-scaled fonts.

Reinterpret:

- Products become case studies.
- Product specifications become project outcomes and methods.
- Media galleries become portfolio previews and case-study dialogs.
- Retail conversion becomes contact conversion.

Replace:

- Brand name, logo, icons, product imagery, marketing copy, and footer legal content.
- Any distinctive source campaign concept or trade dress that is unnecessary to communicate the general design principles.

## Validation Gate

- The new site has a clear owner, audience, and primary action in the first viewport.
- Content architecture fits the new use case rather than mirroring the source.
- All visible assets are original, owned, licensed, or documented placeholders.
- Typography, spacing, color roles, component proportions, and motion form one coherent system.
- Desktop and mobile have intentional layouts, not merely scaled versions.
- No broken images, placeholder data URIs, unreadable text, overlap, or inaccessible controls remain.
- The transfer brief explains why the result belongs to the new person or business.
